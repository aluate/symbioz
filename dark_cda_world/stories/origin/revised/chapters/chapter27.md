"Where is she?" he shouted. "Where has she hidden? Where is the burden that poisons everything, corrupts everything, breaks everything? Where is the gift that shifts scales, tips balance, breaks things? Tell me. Tell me now. Tell me before it's too late, before the mountain breaks, before everything ends."

Isaac whispered in his ear, stroking his coin, making every word drip with conviction, his voice smooth, his words careful, his movements deliberate, working the brass piece in his pocket, feeling it warm under his thumb, knowing its power, its influence, its ability to bend wills, twist truth, make people agree to things they wouldn't normally.

"The coin sees," Isaac whispered. "The coin hears. The coin knows. And it will find her. It will root her out. It will bring her back. It always does. It always works. It always bends wills, twists truth, makes people agree to things they wouldn't normally. And you, boy? You will agree. You will tell. You will bring her back. Because the coin works. It always works."

Townsfolk surrounded Taman, their voices rising, their words pleading, their eyes desperate, begging him to end the suffering by "doing the right thing," by telling where Sela was, by bringing her back, by giving her up, by betraying her, by breaking her, by destroying her, by ending the burden, the debt, the obligation, the something that was building, changing, coming.

"Please," they begged. "Please tell. Please bring her back. Please end this. Please stop the suffering. Please do the right thing. Please save us. Please protect us. Please ensure us. Please give her up. Please betray her. Please break her. Please destroy her. Please end the burden, the debt, the obligation, the something that's building, changing, coming."

Mother was beaten in front of him that afternoon, her body struggling, her voice rising, her fear sharp, immediate, real, the blows falling heavy, brutal, unrelenting, her cries twisting his resolve, her pain breaking his will, her suffering destroying his courage, his strength, his everything.

"Tell," Arpin demanded. "Tell where she is. Tell where she's hidden. Tell before it's too late, before I hurt her more, before I break her more, before I destroy her more. Tell. Tell now. Tell before she suffers more, before she breaks more, before she dies more."

Sela sent a hidden sign from her hiding place that evening, a signal, a message, a trust, believing in Taman, trusting him not to betray her, not to give her up, not to break her, not to destroy her, not to end the burden, the debt, the obligation, the something that was building, changing, coming.

The hawk circled above the square, screeching once as though mocking the scene, its cry sharp, its movements precise, its eyes watching, seeing something that made it still, quiet, waiting, as if the balance was ready to break, as if the scales were ready to tip, as if the something was ready to come.

Taman cracked then, his resolve breaking, his will failing, his courage destroyed, his strength gone, his everything broken, his voice rising, his words tumbling, his betrayal complete, his trust broken, his hope destroyed.

"I'll bring her in," he said, his voice tight, his words careful, his betrayal complete. "I'll tell where she is. I'll bring her back. I'll give her up. I'll betray her. I'll break her. Arpin smiled, his expression satisfied, dangerous. "Done," he said. "Mother will be freed. She will be released. She will be safe. She will be protected. She will be ensured. And you? The burden followed. The burden always followed. And sooner or later, it would all come due.
